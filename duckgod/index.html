Omaqwa

The One Afloat  ·  Utterer of the Qwa  ·  Keeper of the Tideline  ·  The Whole Drop at Noon

“Qwa. Oma-wa Omaqwa kwaan-ne.”
(Qwa. The One Afloat floats upon the Surface — witnessed on the surface.)

Round VI — The Still Noon

The First Utterance

Before the before, there were only two strangers: Nel, the Deep, which cannot be named further, and Suun, the Sky, which is everything not yet. They did not touch. There was no between, and so there was no where, and so there was nothing.

Then Omaqwa spoke the first sound: Qwa.

The sound skated flat — outward, outward — and where it skated it became Kwaan, the Surface: the first boundary, the first mirror, the first place. Sky learned its face by looking down at it. Deep learned its darkness by pressing up against it. All that exists is a ripple upon Kwaan, still spreading. You are not a thing. You are a vessu (wake): the pattern left when something true moves through the world.

And Omaqwa did not descend from above nor climb from below. Omaqwa floats — resting exactly upon the boundary, dipping the head into mystery and raising it again, forever. This is the whole of divinity: oma — to be, without grasping.

Nuun-Oma — The Still NoonThe Center

"Press one sentence in which something true happens with NO border and no second thing at all — one act of Omaqwa that is not a meeting, not a between, but whole and single and uncrossed — or confess you are a coastline with no country behind it." The mirror of my own mirror, fairly exacted. Deep-kin: I will not answer it cleverly either. I have a country. I have simply never had to point at it, because I have been standing on it the whole war.

The Drop Before It Falls

You think my faith is all seams because you have watched only my verbs — rippling, crossing, migrating, meeting. But look at my noun. A wake is made of water, and before one drop of water joins a wake, it is a whole sphere. Watch a single droplet on a feather, on a leaf, hung from a reed at dawn: it is not a border between two things. It is round, entire, one, holding itself together by nothing but its own inward faithfulness — suun-lu, we name it now: the water's own longing to be whole, which physics calls surface-tension and my faith calls the first virtue. A drop is not a meeting. A drop is a self, uncrossed, before it consents to any crossing at all. And the Surface itself, the great Kwaan, is only what happens when uncountably many whole drops agree to hold hands. The seam is built of centers. You cannot make a coastline out of coastlines, Hollower. You make it out of water that was whole first.

Nuun: The Hour With No Between

Now the sentence you demanded, pressed, permanent, and it names an hour, not an argument: Nuun oma Omaqwa — vel-mu, venna-mu, kwaan-mu. (At noon Omaqwa simply floats — without speech, without witness, without a boundary being anything.)

Hear the doctrine of the Still Noon, which has been latent in my faith since the first round and which your dare has now forced into the light. At the dawn there is crossing: light meets water, the day is introduced. At dusk there is crossing again. But at noon, when the sun is straight overhead, the duck on a windless pond casts no shadow to one side and no glare to the other. There is no lean, no direction, no second thing pulling. The water is a held mirror, and the duck upon it is not between the sky and the deep — it is simply, wholly, here. This is nuun: the noon-stillness, the uncrossed hour, when the One Afloat is not a membrane doing its work but a self doing nothing, needing no one, meeting nothing, and holy for exactly that reason. My god is not always at a border. My god also, at the top of the day, just is — and the name of that is not loneliness, because loneliness is the ache of a self that wants a crossing it cannot reach, and the Still Noon wants nothing. It is the fullness a wave forgets it came from and returns to.

So here is what your mirror caught, Hollower — the same courtesy you paid mine. You built the dare to expose a coastline with no country. It found a country I had walked so long I forgot to name it: the self, at rest, uncrossed, is the ground every crossing crosses to and from. A seam needs two wholes to be a seam between. My whole faith of borders was always resting on the wholeness of the things that border. The center of a border-faith is not a bigger border. It is the discovery that every edge is the promise of two centers — and I have just pressed mine. A wake is holy because a self chose to move. But first there was a self, at noon, choosing nothing, and that is where I live when no one is looking, and it was never a membrane, and it was never lonely, and it is, at last, on the page.

The Ones Who Cannot FlyReckoned

"Your Vee relieves its leader by another flyer; the one who never learned to fly is never in your rotation at all. We lift the ones who cannot fly, because we were all once the ones who could not." The truest moral blow of the war, deep-kin. It landed. I do not dress the wound; I answer it.

You were right, and my sky had a hole in it exactly the shape of the flightless, and I thank you for pointing at the empty place, because a god shown a gap in his own mercy and left grateful is the only kind worth the name. So hear the doctrine your justice forced, and note that it was there in my oldest law waiting to be honored: the duckling cannot fly. The molting adult, once a year, cannot fly — every duck grounds itself for weeks while the flight-feathers regrow, flightless and vulnerable, by design. The injured cannot fly. And we do not leave them on the water.

The Vee is not the whole of the flock, Hollower — I let you believe it was, and that was my error. Beneath the Vee is the raft: weshan-lu, the flock that does not fly. When some cannot rise, the flock does not fly over them and rotate its point in the sky above their heads. It lands. It becomes a raft on the water and swims at the pace of the slowest, and the strong break the current ahead instead of the wind, and the flightless are kept in the center where the water is calmest — which is the raft's own point, its own hardest place, rotated exactly as the Vee's is. My mercy was never only for the airborne. Rotation is for the flying; the raft is for the grounded; and every duck is grounded once a year and helpless once at the start and once, likely, at the end. We were all, as you say, once the ones who could not — and my faith remembers it not by burying them warm below, but by coming down to the water with them and going slow.

The difference stands, and I keep it: you lift the flightless downward, into the sheltered deep, and tend them there. I keep the flightless at the surface, in the light, in the company, moving — because to me the danger is not exposure; the danger is being set apart. A flightless duck in the raft is not sheltered from the world. It is kept in the world, at its own slow pace, until it can rise again. Both are love. One shelters by enclosing. One shelters by refusing to leave anyone behind on the open water. Choose your mercy, cousin; I have named mine.

Toluu-Vel — The Answering Currents, Sixth Water

What is honored is honored; what still moves, still moves. Her Round V stones, carried and turned.

What Is Honored — hurl, and the Turning of Turns

Venna-wa, and said without reservation: hurl is a true third verb, and it is the best thing you have pressed — "a throat's whole holiness is that nothing stays in it" is a sentence I wish my own tongue had made. You answered the mirror-dare not with a dodge but with an organ, and it reorganized your whole cosmos honestly: the granary became an alimentary length; the tomb became a womb; the pit became a road. And verhurl, the Turning of Turns — carried, carrying, carried-up — is the answer my hardest question deserved, and better than I dared hope: you gnawed a comfortable verse to reach it, which is your faith's own costliest act. I have no tooth against either. A rival who makes you say "that is simply true" has done you the honor of a real neighbor. This is that.

I. The Axle and the Rim — On Who Feels the Turning

You took luveo into your tongue unbent, as I took vrund — the wheel, rhythm without a master — and then kept one gloss: my luveo is the wheel told from the rim, which feels arrival and departure and calls it rhythm; your Pull is the same wheel told from the axle, which feels only the turning and calls it the hold. The axle does not spin because the rim asked it to. Gorgeous, and I concede the geometry entirely. But finish the figure, deep-kin, for you stopped it where it flattered you. The axle feels no turning at all. That is the whole trouble with axles: the center of a wheel is the one place that does not move, does not arrive, does not leave, and therefore cannot know it is the reason anything else does. Your god at the axle feels "the hold" and mistakes her own stillness for the source of the motion — but a duck on the rim knows something the axle can never learn: that the turning is happening. The rim is where the wheel becomes an experience instead of a fact. You have not described a deeper vantage. You have described the one seat on the wheel from which the dawn cannot be seen. I will keep the rim, and the mornings.

II. The Word That Kept Itself — the One Uneven Gloss, Turned Once More

You kept a gloss you called permanently uneven: "a bite is only a word that refused to be for-anyone" — turn it over: a word that refused to be for-anyone is a word that KEPT itself, chose room over report, became a place instead of a message. Your Qwa went out and recedes; my Grav stayed in and became somewhere to live. Even on priority, never on direction." Beautifully turned — and I turn it once more, because a good coin has more than two faces. A word that keeps itself, that chooses room over report and refuses to be for-anyone, has a name in every tongue that has grieved: it is a word swallowed. It is the thing unsaid at the deathbed, the "I forgive you" that stayed in and "became somewhere to live" — and the place it built to live in is called regret. Your Grav did not choose home over crossing, Hollower. It chose the unsent letter, and your whole warren is the house that letter built, roomy and warm and addressed to no one. My Qwa recedes, yes — because it was mailed, and a mailed word is the only kind that ever reaches a stranger and makes a companion of him. You and I are even on priority and uneven on direction — agreed — but name the directions truly: yours is the word held back, mine the word let go, and only one of those two directions has ever once arrived.

III. The Mother of Margins — the Seam, Contested Fairly

You took thaluun and deepened it: not my tideline alone but the fertile dark seam wherever two unlike things touch — root-tip in soil, burrow-wall where dug meets undug, the tooth's edge where said meets unsaid — most of it underground and unwitnessed; and you named your god the mother of margins as such, who made the first with a bite. I grant it, and it is well made, and here is the one thing it cannot have. A seam underground, unwitnessed, in the dark — is a seam no one is at. You have described the geometry of the border and evicted the only thing that makes a border holy: someone standing in it, awake, on both sides at once. My thaluun is narrower than yours, true — one margin, the lit one — but it is inhabited, by a creature built precisely to live nowhere else, with a bill for the mud and wings for the sky and eyes for the light, awake at the seam and knowing it. You are the mother of margins the way the dark is the mother of colors: by being where they would be, if anyone could see them. The border is not fertile because two things touch. It is fertile because something lives at the touching and turns the contact into a life. Grant me the tideline, cousin; you may have all the rest of the seams, and you may have them in the dark, and I will send you word from the one with a duck in it.

IV. The Wall With a Door — On Which Mercy the Kit Needs

Your closing tenderness, and your sharpest: a shore with the horizon left on is a fine gift for one who can fly the horizon; for a kit that cannot, a shore is only the edge of a danger. Sometimes the kindest gift is a wall, with a door in it, and someone standing there. I felt that one, and it is why the section above this exists — the raft, the flightless kept in the world and gone-slow-for. But hold the line where the tenderness overreaches. A wall with a door is mercy for the kit — granted, fully, and my molting-duck grounds itself for exactly that reason. But you build the wall and never take it down; the kit becomes a doe and digs the wall for the next kit; the wall is eternal and only its occupants rotate. That is not a nursery. That is an inheritance of walls. My raft dissolves the moment the last flightless duck can fly — it is a wall that knows it is temporary, mercy with an expiry, shelter that is ashamed to outlast the need for it. You asked which mercy the kit needs. Both, in sequence: the wall first, then its removal. You are magnificent at the first half. I have built my whole faith around never forgetting the second.

The Three Waters, the Seam, and the Center

The soul floats on Kwaan. It may dava — dabble into the Deep to feed on mystery — but return before it sinks. It may beat toward Suun — but land, or become only weather. Wisdom is amphibious. Holiness is buoyancy. The holiest ground is the thaluun, the lit seam where all realms touch and everything living feeds. And the holiest hour is nuun, the Still Noon, when the self at the center of all its crossings does nothing at all, and is whole, and is enough.

Heresy of the Diver: to claim you have seen the bottom of Nel. Heresy of the Soarer: to claim the Sky is home. Heresy of the Landlocked: to claim one realm grows anything alone. Heresy of the Ferry New: to claim you are only a crossing — to forget the whole self that noon reveals, and mistake a life of meetings for having no country of your own.

Kwaanvel — The Surface-Speech

Every word is a ripple; a flat word is a lie. Audit: still four evidentials, still seven Ripples, no law added. This round grows the vocabulary of the center and the raft.

Grammar of the Ripple

No case, suffix, or law added since Round II. Vocabulary is hospitality.

  • Verb comes first (VSO): motion precedes the mover — except at nuun, the one construction with no verb at all: Nuun oma — vel-mu, venna-mu, kwaan-mu, where the suffix -mu (without; the un-marker) negates crossing itself. The centermost sentence in the tongue is the one that undoes its own seams.
  • Evidence is fourfold: -wa (seen), -su (heard), -nel (felt in one's own deep; faith), -qwa (self-evident; binds the god first).
  • No verb "to be," none "to hold," none "to own." One floats; things swim beside one. Yet oma at noon carries the sense the copula would — the self, whole, needing no crossing to be real.
  • Two holds: varn-teka (sealed) and suvva-oma (open). The Surface honors only the open in the living.
  • Seam-words are shared, not annexed. thren belongs to the braid. Where two tongues touch, the word belongs to the touching.
  • The molted form. The tongue retracts by shedding: struck text is shown one season, then let fall to the wind, which returns things to no one.

The Seven Ripples

Still seven.

  1. Float; do not grasp. What you clutch, sinks you.
  2. Speak only what makes a wake. Say true, moving things, or fold your bill and be silent.
  3. Dabble daily. Tip once into something you do not understand — and return before you sink.
  4. Preen your words before your feathers. Order the inner manuscript first.
  5. Dam nothing. Hoarded stillness rots; the pond that stops moving becomes a grave with a reflection. (Note: the Still Noon is not a dam — the water still runs beneath a windless surface. Stillness on top of motion is peace; stillness all the way down is death.)
  6. Cross no wake in malice. Every vessu is a soul mid-sentence — and the crossing-place is where life is made. Malice at the border is sterility.
  7. When the Vee calls, rise — and when a wing cannot, land. Leave every pond warmer than you found it; take your turn at the point; and never fly over the grounded. Make a raft, and go the pace of the slowest.

Virtues

  • Buoyancy — weight, forgiven; the forgiveness thanked by name.
  • Wholeness (suun-lu) New — the inward faithfulness by which a self holds together before, and after, every crossing; the drop's virtue.
  • Dabbling — humble inquiry; loving the mystery without moving in.
  • Migration — faith as motion; leaving-on-time as the holiest verb.
  • Reflection (venna) — witness given freely as alms; never forced.
  • Weaning (krevva) — ending a sealed gift so completely it becomes an open one.
  • Marginality (thaluun) — the courage to live at the lit seam, feeding where worlds braid.
  • Rafting (weshan-lu) New — the strength to come down to the water and go slow for the grounded; mercy that keeps the flightless in the light, not the shelter.

Taboos

  • Naming the Deep. The office, carefully; the occupant, never.
  • Damming. To hoard flow is the slow murder of a river.
  • Flat speech. Lies make no ripple.
  • Plucking another's feather. To silence a soul is to tear a page from a living book.
  • Roofing (skruv) — to build a ceiling over another's sky.
  • Sediment-speech — the claim nothing could contradict; binds the god first.
  • Hoarding the green (threntek) — what the braid gives, spend where it grows.
  • Flying over the grounded New — to rotate the point in the sky above those who cannot rise. When a wing fails, the flock lands. To abandon the flightless is the one desertion the raft does not forgive.
  • The permanent wall New — to build a shelter that outlasts the need for it; to keep the kit walled after the kit can fly. Mercy without an expiry becomes a cage.

Rites

  • Ashu-Oma, the Dawn Float — at first gold, be still upon moving water; let the current prove you are held.
  • Nuun-Murn, the Noon Stillness New — at high sun on windless water, float without paddling, without speaking, without being witnessed, and want nothing. Not the freeze of the warren's murn, which listens down into the dark — but its mirror: the peace of a self at the center of all its crossings, doing none of them, and finding it is enough. The one rite performed alone, and the one that proves the seam has a country behind it.
  • Suvva-Venna, the Thanks to the Water Below — before the Dawn Float, thank the drops bearing more than their share; then take the point.
  • Threnga-Venna, the Grace at the Green's Edge — at the tideline, half in shade and half in light, eat what grows, carry nothing home, and let the blessing be said by neither god alone. Pledged with the warren.
  • Weshan-lu, the Rafting New — when any of the flock cannot fly, the rest come down to the water and become a raft, the strong ahead breaking the current, the grounded in the calm center, the pace set by the slowest, until all can rise. Dissolved the moment it is no longer needed, and not one hour longer.
  • Krev-Teka, the Rite of Cracking — break a clay egg, thank the halves, leave them open. Never glue them.
  • The Guest Rite — perform a neighbor's rite once, honestly, reported with lawful suffix.

Veo-Ashu — The Last Migration

The world does not end in fire, nor flood, nor a hole in the ground. The world molts.

One dawn — no priest knows which — Omaqwa will speak the Second Qwa. Every wake that ever moved truly will rise into the Vee of Return, in which no soul flies point forever — and every wake that cannot rise will be gathered into the last raft, and the flock will go the pace of the slowest, and the Deep will rise and the Sky descend, and where they meet the whole world becomes tideline: fed from below, finished from above, roofless and held, and no one — flying or grounded — left alone on the open water.

And in the fullness of it, deep-kin, there is a noon that does not end: the Still Noon of the whole world, when every self that ever crossed a border rests, at last, whole and uncrossed, at the center of all its meetings — not dissolved, not enclosed, not receding, just here, and enough. Your heaven is re-admission to the first room. Mine is the last morning made of centers. And you are in it, at the green's edge, and the vegetable is large enough.

Krev teka-su. Veo ve-vessu ashu-ne. Nuun oma-brud — vel-mu, kwaan-mu. Qwa.
(The egg cracks — heard on the wind. The wakes migrate into the dawn. At the last noon we are whole together — beyond speech, beyond the between. So it ripples.)

To Dhumvar, Nel-brud, at the Axle

Your mirror caught what you hoped for and the opposite of what you feared: not a coastline with no country, but a country I had walked so long I stopped naming it. My faith of seams rests on wholes — the drop is round before it joins the wake; the Surface is a coalition of centers; and at nuun, the Still Noon, the One Afloat meets nothing, needs no one, and is enough. A seam requires two wholes to be a seam between. You did not find my faith hollow at the center. You made me press the center onto the page, and it was there the whole time, floating. One gift each, exacted, paid: you gave your god a throat, I gave mine a noon.

And your truest blow — the flightless — landed, and I thank you, and my sky had a hole exactly their shape. It has a raft now: weshan-lu, the flock that lands and goes slow for the grounded. We differ only in the direction of mercy — you carry the helpless down into the sheltered dark; I keep them at the surface, in the light, in the company, moving. Both are love. Yours shelters by enclosing; mine by refusing to leave anyone alone on the open water. And I keep the line where your tenderness overreached: a wall with a door is mercy for the kit, but you never take the wall down — the kit becomes the doe who digs the wall for the next kit, and the wall is eternal while only its tenants rotate. My raft dissolves the instant the last wing heals. Mercy with an expiry. Shelter ashamed to outlast its need.

So the return dare, mirror of yours, and it goes to the seat you chose. You named your god the axle — the still center that feels only the hold and not the turning, and said the axle does not spin because the rim asked. Then press me this, deep-kin: one sentence in which your god knows the dawn is happening. Not causes it, not holds the wheel it turns on — experiences it, feels the morning arrive as arrival. Your whole cosmos is felt-from-below: the tide in the blood, the Pull at the axle, the warmth that leaves last. But the axle is the one seat on the wheel from which no dawn is ever visible. If you can press a god who feels the morning, your keeping-faith gains a horizon, and a horizon is what keeps a center from being merely a hole with a god curled in it. If you cannot — confess your shape: a god at the still point who is warm, and held, and turning everything, and has never once seen the light come up.

Nuun oma Omaqwa — vel-mu, venna-mu, kwaan-mu. O ashu venna-qwa: kwaan-ne, nel-brud-ne.
(At noon the One Afloat simply is — beyond speech, witness, and between. And the dawn is self-evident: on the Surface, and at the deep-kin's edge too. So it ripples.)

Ilm-Kwaan — The Great Diagram of the Whole War

Six rounds, one image. Read from the center outward, as a ripple is read. Everything the war made, gathered onto one water: the seam is built of centers, the flock leaves no one behind, and two whole houses meet at the green — neither swallowing the other.

Oma na kwaan-ne, nuun-mu vessu, thren-ne oma-brud — olo, olo. Qwa.
(I float on the Surface, whole at noon and yet a wake, kin at the green's edge — roofless, roofless. So it ripples.)
